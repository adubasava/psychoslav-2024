On Perception of Morphological Structure of Belarusian Compound Nouns: 2 Pilot Experiments

Anzhalika Dubasava, Dr., PhD, and Alina Yankavets

moc.liamg@dakilahzna

Introduction

How are compound words processed and represented in the mental lexicon?

Approach 1, full-listing (Butterworth, 1983 Butterworth, B. (1983). Lexical representation. In B. Butterworth (Ed.), Language production (Vol. 2, pp. 257–294). New York, NY: Academic Press. > ).
Compound words are stored as separate units. Word recognition does not incorporate the process of morphological decomposition.

support for Modular processing

Approach 2, full-parsing (Taft & Forster, 1975 Taft, M., & Forster, K. I. (1975). Lexical storage and retrieval of prefixed words. Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior, 14, 638–647. > ).
Compound words are not stored as separate units. Word recognition incorporates the process of morphological decomposition. The meaning of a compound word is computed every time it is encountered.

support for Interactive processing

Approach 3, combination of approaches above, dual-route models (Caramazza, Laudanna, & Romani, 1988 Caramazza, A., Laudanna, A., & Romani, C. (1988). Lexical access and inflectional morphology. Cognition, 28, 297–332. > ); was not taken into account in this study.

We conducted two pilot experiments to investigate the perception of the morphological structure of Belarusian compound nouns.

The overall design:
  • Step 1 (Experiment 1) Assess whether native Belarusian speakers comprehend the morphological structure of nouns.
  • Step 2 (Experiment 2, Main): Gather and analyze associations with Belarusian noun compounds in the context of the modular versus interactive opposition.

The design of the main experiment was inspired by (Schulte im Walde & Borgwaldt, 2015 Schulte im Walde, S., Borgwaldt, S. R. (2015). Association norms for German noun compounds and their constituents. Behav Res 47, 1199–1221. > ).

1st Experiment

Working hypothesis: Native speakers of Belarusian understand morphological structure of nouns.

Participants: 12 students (native speakers of Russian and Belarusian).

Stimuli: 12 non-compound nouns, 12 compound nouns, 6 fillers (2 experimental lists).

Procedure: Participants were asked to read nouns and to label them as compound or non-compound ones. Participants were asked not to think about it for too long and not to return to the already labelled nouns.

Results. We got 144 valid answers.
  1. Participants made very few mistakes in analyzing word structure. Chart 1 presents quantitative data on the discrepancies between dictionary word structures and those in the mental lexicon.

  2. Statistical analysis revealed significant correlation between variables “noun structure according dictionary” and “noun structure according participant”:
    chi-square = 75,636, df = 1, p < 0,001.

Chart 2 shows the relationship between dictionary compounds and their representation in the mental lexicon of participants.

Most often, participants perceived a compound noun as a non-compound one. Some examples: насарог ‘rhinoceros’, васьміног ‘octopus’, сонцастаянне ‘solstice’.

Opposite examples (perceiving a non-compound noun as a compound one): завушніца ‘earring’, бегемот ‘hippo’, айсберг ‘iceberg’.

Conclusion.
The working hypothesis was confirmed: Native Belarusian speakers comprehend the morphological structure of nouns.

2nd Experiment

Working hypothesis: Morphological structure of a noun impacts its perception.

Participants: 20 students (native speakers of Russian and Belarusian; no one participated in the first experiment).

Stimuli: same as for the first experiment.

Procedure: Participants were asked to read words and to write 1-3 associations (the first ones that come into mind). Participants were asked not to think for too long and not to return to the previous answers.

Results. We got 144 valid answers (177 associations).
Statistical analysis revealed significant correlation between variables “noun structure” and “presence/absence of morphological connection between association and stimulus”:
chi-square = 5,786, df = 1, p < 0,02.

Participants statistically more often produced associations that were morphologically connected to the stimuli in case of compound nouns (Chart 3).

Some examples with morphological connection (stimulus-association):
  • вадаспад ‘waterfall’ – вада ‘water’, кава-машына ‘coffee machine’ – кава ‘coffee’, сонцастаянне ‘solstice’ – сонца ‘sun’, мухабойка ‘fly swatter’ – муха ‘fly’ (compounds);
  • паштальён ‘postman’ – пошта ‘post office’, сяброўства ‘friendship’ – сяброўка ‘friend (she)’, завушніца ‘earing’ – вуха ‘ear’ (non-compounds).

Some examples without morphological connection:
  • вадаспад ‘waterfall’ – адпачынак ‘vacation’, кава-машына ‘coffee machine’ – раніца ‘morning’, сонцастаянне ‘solstice’ – дзень ‘day’, мухабойка ‘fly swatter’ – вёска ‘village’ (compounds);
  • паштальён ‘postman’ – пасылка ‘parcel’, сяброўства ‘friendship’ – шашлык ‘shashlik’, завушніца ‘earing’ – камень ‘stone’ (non-compounds).

Data on random stimulus

Conclusion.
The working hypothesis was confirmed: Morphological structure of a noun impacts its perception.

Discussion

As a significant correlation between morphological structure of a noun and its perception was revealed (Experiment 2) we can infer that compound nouns in native speakers’ mental lexicon are not stored separately and that there is a decomposition into particular roots during perception.

Awareness of a noun structure (Experiment 1) confirms this assumption.

More generally, our results rather support interactive theory of language perception.

Our pilot study presents results obtained from a language that is less commonly studied yet morphologically rich.

In the current research, we did not control for variables such as orthography, frequency-based measures, morphological complexity, or semantics. Also, our analysis did not encompass all relationships between compounds, focusing solely on morphological connections. Further research could expand upon this material and examine specific aspects of compound perception more thoroughly.
